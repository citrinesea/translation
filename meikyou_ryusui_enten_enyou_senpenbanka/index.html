November 28, 2025
Ibuki's entrance into AKATSUKI has insensitive parallels to the history between Japan and the Ryukyu Islands, specifically the annexation of Ryukyu into Okinawa from 1872-1879 onward. Click for more information

AKATSUKI is a unit embodied by 和 (wa, lit. harmony, but also a self-designator for Japan). The word 和風 (wafu, Japanese style) comes from 和. During the Meiji Restoration, 和 was central to Japan’s efforts to unify and assimilate new territories under a shared cultural identity. On top of his membership, Ibuki's active pursuit of 本当の和風 (true Japanese-style) being the reason he pursues AKATSUKI is inappropriate, bearing this in mind.

Additionally, the Ryukyu Disposition was driven by military interests, particularly securing Japan's southern borders from other nations. In Okinawan schools, national pride was linked to serving Japan’s military goals. This had devastating consequences, particularly during the Battle of Okinawa in World War II. This in mind, having Ibuki join the self-proclaimed "war unit" is also inappropriate.

Production Notes

Two new songs have been made for the AKATSUKI Fan Thanksgiving and Starphony Concert. These two songs express the musical range of the reborn AKATSUKI. "Meikyou Ryusui," released the other day, is a song tranquilly sung, that uses an abundance of traditional Japanese instrumentation in line with AKATSUKI’s traditional Japanese style. While "Enten Enyou Senpenbanka" definitively uses Japanese scales, it was created without the use of any traditional Japanese instruments. It is also the first song where AKATSUKI has tried to attempt “precise" harmonies.

Interpretation Notes

Section Summary: The three FanThanx songs are a unified project, made so that the original AKATSUKI can ask themselves the question "What is true Japanese-style?" Musically, this is done through different combinations of Ibuki's Western influence (EDM) and Japanese scales and instrumentation in a blended dynamic resembling 和魂洋才 (wakon yōsai; Japanese Spirit, Western Technology), the motivation for Westernization Japan experienced during the Meiji Restoration.

As I've established in Unpacking Shinsei (新生) AKATSUKI, AKATSUKI's integration of Ibuki directly parallels wakon yōsai: his American influence represents the “western technology” adopted strategically to ensure AKATSUKI’s survival and evolution. Keito and Souma explicitly frame his integration as essential for the unit to survive change, avoid control from traditionalist forces, and lead to a transformation.

This framing reflects the logic of wakon yōsai during the Meiji Era. At first, the philosophy was meant to preserve tradition while adopting foreign tools.

"Certainly, the Meiji slogan wakon yōsai (和魂洋才 Japanese spirit and Western learning) demonstrates that modernization did not mean the elimination of tradition. Quite the contrary, the initial desire [...] was to keep Japanese ideas and concepts along with Western technologies."
Zohar & Miller, page 8 (2020)

However, as Zohar & Miller (2020) notes, this desire to keep spirit and technology separate actually led to a hybrid form, where Japanese influence and American influence combined.

"Certainly, the Meiji slogan wakon yōsai (和魂洋才 Japanese spirit and Western learning) demonstrates that modernization did not mean the elimination of tradition. Quite the contrary, the initial desire, that in the end failed to perform as originally expected, was to keep Japanese ideas and concepts along with Western technologies. Instead, Meiji thought reconciles both, creating a new hybrid form which resides in-between the spirit and technologies of Japan and the West."
Zohar & Miller, page 8 (2020)

The Search for True Japanese-style

Ibuki's current motivation in joining AKATSUKI is searching for "true Japanese-style." Judging by the direction and production of these three songs, it seems as if his presence also catalyzes original AKATSUKI to begin their own search. The three FanThanx songs, written by each member, seem to be their individual answers: three different visions of "Japanese-style" using different balances of Western and Japanese elements.

Before diving in, it's important to reiterate a few things from Unpacking Shinsei (新生) AKATSUKI.

A search for change and true Japanese-style on AKATSUKI's part does NOT negate the problematic parallels to assimilation Ibuki's personal search for true Japanese-style has; these two journeys are happening simultaneously and are interconnected. AKATSUKI’s modernization is powered by Western influence as they take on Ibuki’s American way of thinking, while it protects its core Japanese identity. This leaves Ibuki as the one taking on Japanese-style high-context behavior. This imbalance is what parallels the Ryukyu Disposition.
Ibuki's search for "true Japanese-style" mirrors the problematic logic of historical assimilation, where Okinawan identity is only validated through conformity to mainland standards. This is worsened by the fact it is voluntary; this erases the real colonial power dynamics and pressures to assimilate that existed during the Ryukyu Disposition.

To the original AKATSUKI, the answer to "What is true Japanese-style?" seems to be a Japanese-style that withstands the changing times, inherent to life. This is reflected in the Buddhist philosophy of impermanence (諸行無常) that is consistently prevalent AKATSUKI songs and stories. As Enten Enyou Senpenbanka states, AKATSUKI aims to create a waypoint, or an ideal to hold them steady, in a world that is constantly changing. Like a guiding star, this seems to be the "true Japanese-style" the original AKATSUKI has now begun to search for.

Acknowledging the change in their lives, Souma reiterates AKATSUKI's search as, "Just as dynamic as water and fire, AKATSUKI will continue to question what kind of existence it is." Fire and water are complete opposites, yet they both share the same state of incorporeality and constant change. Flames flicker, rage and die. Water is still and rushing. They have no defined shape that can be captured. The lyrics in Meikyou Ryusui and Enten Enyou Senpenbanka line up with this directly. As the production note states, they are meant to represent the range of Japanese-style AKATSUKI is capable of accomplishing, and capture AKATSUKI's evolving identity.

Meikyou Ryusui
Keito's song lies closest to the "wakon" polar end, or Japanese-style older AKATSUKI songs are known for. It uses a high amount of Japanese instrumentation, and a high amount of pentatonic scales. The Western influence is light, reflected in the subtle trap beat throughout the song. Its additional theme is water. Keito's lyrics are poetic.

LOUDEST BUGS
Souma's song is a balanced fusion of "wakon" and "yōsai". The song begins with blending Western and Japanese influence, with Japanese instruments used to create EDM samples (e.g., taiko stick clicks, shamisen, shakuhachi). It also incorporates a moderate use of pentatonic scales. Its additional theme is wind. Souma uses complex phrasing in his lyrics, but also incorporates English suggestions from Ibuki.

Enten Enyou Senpenbanka
Kuro's song lies closest to the "yōsai" polar end. The song ultimately relies on the use of pentatonic scales for its Japanese-style influence, and expressly avoids using traditional Japanese instrumentation (only consisting of syntheizer and piano). Its additional theme is fire.

Together, the three FanThanx songs feel like a Ship of Theseus experimentation: to what extent does the existence or absence of instrumentation and pentatonic scales make something Japanese-style? As you take away components like "traditional Japanese instruments" or the use of pentatonic scales characteristic of "Japanese-style music," and at what point does it stop being "Japanese-style"?
