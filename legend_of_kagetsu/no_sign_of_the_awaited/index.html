Translation Notes

  1. ↑ Souma's running gag is regularly resorting to performing seppuku to make up for trouble. 切腹 (seppuku or harakiri, lit. belly-cutting ) was a form of ritualistic suicide samurai used for a variety of reasons. For Souma, that tends to be shame. However, in this context, it is an act that asks for a true, heartfelt plea to be heard.
  2. ↑ In Japanese, words from other languages (such as English) are typically rendered in katakana. However, when Souma uses katakana words, they are all written in hiragana instead, likely to emphasize how he struggles with foreign words. These words are kept as written in hiragana, and put in "quotes".
  3. ↑ Referencing the story Shinsengumi, where AKATSUKI, MaM and others put together a show in Keito's hometown.
  4. ↑ The flashback scene in Shinsengumi shows Souma after his entrance exam with AKATSUKI.
  5. ↑ 報連相 (horenso, lit. "reporting, contacting, consulting"), which is about keeping your supervisor informed and aware. It's an intentional homophone with 菠薐草 (hōrensō, “spinach”). I localized this to try and preserve the spinach pun.
  6. ↑ Said in English.
  7. ↑ Souma uses おぬし for this second-person pronoun, which is used for inferiors.
  8. ↑ The Happy End Problem is an unsolvable geometry problem.
  9. ↑ Iado is a martial art that focuses on drawing the sword.
  10. ↑ シーサー (shiisaa) are lion dogs. They originate from Chinese guardian lion traditions and Okinawan folklore. They're usually placed in pairs on rooftops or gates to protect homes and ward off evil spirits.
  11. ↑ Souma says 獅子 (shishi, lion) here.
  12. ↑ Ikkyu-san (一休さん) refers to the Zen monk Ikkyu Sōjun (1394–1481), who is the subject of a popular series of Japanese folktales. In these tales, Ikkyu is portrayed as a clever, mischievous boy monk who outwits adults with his wit and logic. In one story, Ikkyu is asked to "catch the tiger in a painting". Ikkyu responds by asking the adults to make the tiger come out of the painting so he can catch it.
  13. ↑ From Sun Tzu's the Art of War.
  14. ↑ Haisai is an uchinaaguchi greeting used in Okinawa. Uchinaaguchi is the native language of Okinawa, and all uchinaaguchi phrases are kept as written.
  15. ↑ The essence of what Keito is saying is that etiquette means thinking about how others might feel, and acting in a way that shows you care. You're expressing kindness and consideration through your actions, not your words.
  16. ↑ This is written out with the kanji 天翔華月.
  17. ↑ Ibuki sounds this out in hiragana てんしょうかげつ.
  18. ↑ 真実の和風 or true Japanese-style. Read more about the problematic implications this has for Ibuki and AKATSUKI here.
