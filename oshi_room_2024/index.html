In this campaign, you get to narrow down your favorite idols to a singular "oshi": your favorite. The following translation has: the keywords and filters used for the idol selection screen, Q&A responses and charts, and the phone calls you receive after choosing your 3 top idols. Additionally, the "Surging Wave Invitation Campaign" classifications are added to the filter (watch the videos here).

Translation Notes

  1. ↑ Radio calisthenics (ラジオ体操 lit., "radio exercises") are warm-up exercises performed to music and guidance from radio broadcasts.
  2. ↑ This is TPO (time, place, and occasion): there is a time and place for everything. For Souma, that's about being mindful of social expectations before he pulls out his sword. Adonis explains it to him in Resurrection Sunday
  3. ↑ Chilled pineapple on a stick. It can look like this or this.
  4. ↑ Umibouzu (海坊主) is a mythical sea youkai that breaks ships or drowns sailors.
  5. ↑ Tan tan ramen is a spicy ramen dish, based off of Chinese Dan Dan Noodles (Tan tan uses soup broth, while Dan Dan uses sauce).
  6. ↑ Abura soba is a soupless ramen dish, made of oil and soba (buckwheat noodles).
  7. ↑ Referencing Tetora's FS1 4* story Midnight Encounter. You can find it on the masterlist here
  8. ↑ In Japan, summer vacation homework comes in variety; from completing workbooks, to doing a creativity project.
  9. ↑ Osechi dishes are tradtional Japanese New Years foods that are eaten for good health, longevity, safety in the home and good luck.
  10. ↑ Datemaki is a Japanese sweet rolled omelette of eggs, fish cake, and honey for osechi dishes.
  11. ↑ Takigyou, or waterfall meditation/training, where you refresh and purify your spirit standing underneath the cold waters of a waterfall. This was a rare practice that samurai and ascetic Japanese Buddhists and Shintoists engaged in for centuries
  12. ↑ Tetora is referring to this karate activity, done in the New Year’s.
  13. ↑ Chiaki is likely referring to the Hakone Ekiden, an annual long-distance relay right after the New Year’s. The athletes come from various universities in Japan
  14. ↑ Kombu is kelp, used in osechi dishes (New Year’s food) as a wish for good health (in this case, longevity and joy). See more here.
