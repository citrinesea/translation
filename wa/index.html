Unpacking Shinsei (新生) AKATSUKI; An Extended Translation Note and Essay

April 13, 2025

Introduction

The essay below provides more information and necessary context about the issues of cultural sensitivity regarding Ibuki's inclusion in a Japanese-style unit as a native Ryukyuan. These materials will be weaved throughout future Shinsei AKATSUKI translations.

I am not a historian, nor an expert in international relations, nor an authority on everything I speak about below. I have deep-read on these topics. This essay is meant to contextualize Ryukyu/Okinawa's history with Japan and apply it to Ensemble Stars (through translation notes, disclaimers, etc.) I have all of my sources linked in the timeline piece, as well as here, so I encourage you to read beyond what I have here.

Information may change here as more stories come out. I'll designate those updates at the bottom.

The History of Wa

Origins

Wa is the oldest attested name of Japan and ethnonym of the Japanese people. From c. the 2nd century AD Chinese and Korean scribes used the Chinese character 倭; 'submissive', 'distant', 'dwarf' to refer to the various inhabitants of the Japanese archipelago, although it might have been just used to transcribe the phonetic value of a Japonic ethnonym with a respectively differing semantic connotation. In the 8th century, the Japanese started using the character 和, wa, 'harmony', 'peace', 'balance' instead due to the offensive nature of the former.

In current Japanese usage, Wa 倭 "old name for Japan" is a variant Chinese character for Wa 和 "Japan", excepting a few historical terms like the Five kings of Wa, wakō (Chinese Wōkòu 倭寇 "Japanese pirates"), and Wamyō Ruijushō dictionary. In marked contrast, Wa 和 is a common adjective in Sino-Japanese compounds like Washoku 和食 "Japanese cuisine", Wafuku 和服 "Japanese clothing", Washitsu 和室 "Japanese-style room", Waka 和歌 "Japanese-style poetry", Washi 和紙 "traditional Japanese paper", Wagyu 和牛 "Japanese cattle".

Use of wa in nationalism during the Meiji Restoration

Meiji Restoration, in Japanese history, the political revolution in 1868 that brought about the final demise of the Tokugawa shogunate (military government)—thus ending the Edo (Tokugawa) period (1603–1867)—and, at least nominally, returned control of the country to direct imperial rule under Mutsuhito (the emperor Meiji). In a wider context, however, the Meiji Restoration of 1868 came to be identified with the subsequent era of major political, economic, and social change—the Meiji period (1868–1912)—that brought about the modernization and Westernization of the country.

During the Meiji Era (1868-1912), Japanese nationalism arose as a key aspect of the modernization and Westernization efforts, fueled by a desire to become a world power and resist Western colonization. What was Japan? It is tied together by 和. A national identity needed to be created in order to impose it on others 3. Kokutai (National Polity):
The Meiji government developed the concept of "kokutai," which emphasized the unique and special characteristics of Japan and its people.
This concept was used to instill a sense of national pride and to differentiate Japan from other nations.

The education system played a key role in promoting nationalism, with students being taught about the emperor, national history, and the importance of loyalty to the state. We'll talk about this below in A brief history of Ryukyu

Culture Now

https://culturallyours.com/2018/12/05/exploring-the-japanese-culture-of-wa-and-harmony/

Harmony can be found everywhere in Japan. Whether it’s the clean lines and understated nature of Japanese architecture or the orderly manner in which a Japanese meal is arranged on a plate, the concept of “wa” or “harmony” is at the heart of Japanese culture. Shaped by a rich history and deep traditions, harmony informs all aspects of Japanese law and customs.

As a guiding principal in all interactions, whether in a family, social or business environment, wa stresses interdependence over independence, cooperation over dissent, and patience over resistance. It’s why Japan is often considered a culture of conflict-avoidance, and to some, Japanese may be characterized as indecisive, reserved or cautious.

But don’t assume that, just because they act and speak to preserve wa, Japanese don’t have dissenting thoughts or get frustrated. It’s just that achieving wa is so important to Japan’s social values, people distinguish between “hone,” one’s true feelings, and “tatemai,” the face one wears in public. While Western culture may view this as hypocrisy, Japanese understand that rising above one’s personal feelings for the good of society as a whole is its own virtue – you’re not a hypocrite, you’re a good citizen

Parallels between Ibuki joining AKATSUKI and the Ryukyu Disposition

Ibuki's "pursuit of wa" (Final Ceremony, KAGETSU) is bad, because he is actively seeking out "Japanese" style

Ibuki’s “pursuit of wa”, thus, is inherently culturally insensitive, and there is no way of divorcing that from the current premise and circumstances of him joining AKATSUKI.

The following are more of my own personal takes. There are likely more than I list here, and more to find, but these are the overarching problems I see.

Assimilation

The primary issue of AKATSUKI taking in a Ryukyuan into a unit that is ostensibly Japanese-styled.
Ibuki talks about absorbing "wa"/harmony in KAGETSU. While Keito equally talks about taking on American "wa", it does not lessen the matter of Ibuki being said to have "taken on" wa.

Militaristic Pride

This is another large, glaring issue to me in having Ibuki join AKATSUKI. AKATSUKI is established as a "war unit". Ibuki is a combative person. On one hand, the character Ibuki being introduced with the characters in AKATSUKI "makes sense", in that they are also combative. But that seamlessness is soured by the parallels with the Disposition.

The main reason Japan went through with taking the islands was for military gain; it was a massive security risk for Japan to leave them open, should another country come and take them over. Thus, bringing Ibuki into AKATSUKI and connecting them through is something that can't be overlooked to me.

Rising Sun Imagery

I'm uncertain how intentional this is, but I feel the need to point it out because it is prevalent. Shinsei AKATSUKI is characterized in two ways: the four phases of the moon and a rising dawn of new beginnings. AKATSUKI gaining a new member could signify new beginnings, but the context of the Ryukyu Disposition looms over it.

Stepping back: why might this storyline be happening in the first place? An aside on propaganda in media.

Ensemble Stars is not the only media that is incorporating Okinawan characters, settings and themes.

Currently, China is trying to dissassmeble Okinawa from Japan as a military outpost. Regardless of your opinons, there is incentive for Japan to assert Okinawa as a "Japanese" entity. This rise in in media may be an aftereffect of that.
